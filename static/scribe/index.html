    Billing-specific essentials (say these for ANY billing prompt)

    Money = integer minor units

    Store cents as BIGINT + a currency code. Never float. Round once, at a defined point, with an explicit policy; track remainders.

    Idempotency everywhere

    Client idempotency key on writes; vendor idempotency key to the PSP; dedup on event id for webhooks. The whole domain is "make retries safe."

    Append-only audit

    Ledger entries, invoice state transitions, subscription changes — all immutable history. Auditors and finance need "what happened and when."

    Strong consistency for money

    Balance checks & ledger postings are linearizable / single-shard ACID. Eventual is fine for usage rollups, notifications, analytics.

    Reconciliation, always

    Never trust the PSP's webhooks alone. A pull-based job compares your state machine vs the PSP's record of truth and heals drift.

    PCI scope minimization

    Card data goes browser→Stripe directly (Elements). You store a token, never the PAN. Touching raw cards explodes PCI scope.

    Proration — the math they will probe

    Customer on Pro Team adds 2 seats 10 days into a 30-day monthly cycle at $13/seat/mo.

    Remaining fraction = days_left / days_in_period = 20/30 = 0.667.
    Prorated charge = 2 seats × $13 × 0.667 = $17.34 (1734 cents).
    The general rule: credit the unused portion of the old quantity/price, charge the prorated portion of the new. On a pure seat-add the old-quantity credit is $0, so you just charge the new seats' remainder.

    DecisionOptions & tradeoff
    When to bill the prorationImmediately (separate small invoice now) = clearest, but lots of tiny charges. Roll to next invoice (add proration line items to the upcoming invoice) = fewer charges, what Stripe does by default, better UX. Recommend roll-to-next, immediate for upgrades crossing a big amount.
    Time basisWall-clock seconds is most accurate; whole-day is simpler and what most SaaS shows. Pick day-granularity and say why (matches what the customer sees on the invoice).
    Removal creditRefund to card (cash out — finance hates surprise refunds) vs account credit applied to future invoices (recommended; this is a negative line item / credit balance).
    RoundingRound each line item to the cent with a consistent policy (half-up). Never let summed rounded lines disagree with the rounded total — round the lines, then the invoice total is their sum.

    Senior signal: proration is a pure function of (price version, old qty, new qty, time-in-period). Because prices are versioned and immutable, you can always recompute a past proration deterministically — that's what makes disputes and audits answerable.

    Architecture — the recurring billing run

    A scheduled job ("invoice cron") wakes up and finds subscriptions whose
current_period_end <= now:

  Scheduler (per-minute) ── finds due subscriptions
        │
        ▼
  For each due subscription, in ONE Postgres txn:
    1. Generate invoice (status=draft) + line items (base seats, proration,
       credits) from OUR subscription state — NOT from Stripe
    2. Finalize invoice (status=open) — now immutable
    3. Insert outbox_event "invoice.finalized"
        │
        ▼
  Outbox poller ──▶ queue (Amazon MQ / SQS) ──▶ Charge worker
        │
        ▼
  Charge worker:
    - create Stripe PaymentIntent with idempotency_key = invoice_id+attempt
    - on success → mark invoice paid, advance period_end, append event
    - on failure → mark past_due, schedule dunning retry
        │
        ▼
  Stripe webhook (payment_intent.succeeded/.failed) ──▶ reconcile
    (verify sig, dedup event id, REFETCH intent, converge invoice state)

    Two correctness pillars here: (1) generating the invoice and finalizing it and emitting the charge event happen in one DB transaction + outbox — no dual-write. (2) The charge worker passes an idempotency key to Stripe so a retried run never double-charges. Your Zendesk silent-200 bug is exactly the failure this prevents — call that out if it fits.

    Upgrades vs downgrades — the asymmetry to volunteer

    • Upgrade (more seats / higher plan): apply immediately + charge proration now (or next invoice). Entitlement turns on at once. Customer is paying you more — low risk to provision optimistically.
    • Downgrade (fewer seats / lower plan): usually apply at period end, not immediately — otherwise you owe a refund and they keep access they paid for. Schedule the change; bill the new rate next cycle. Volunteer this asymmetry; it shows product sense, not just plumbing.

    Failure modes (volunteer before asked)

    • Charge worker runs twice (queue redelivery): Stripe idempotency key on the PaymentIntent → second attempt returns the original result, no double charge.
    • Process dies after Stripe charges but before we mark paid: webhook + reconciliation job converge the invoice to paid by refetching the intent. State machine is driven by truth from Stripe, not by our optimistic write.
    • Seat added during the exact moment the invoice is being generated: generate from a consistent snapshot (the subscription row + items as of the txn); the seat add either lands in this invoice or the next, never half.
    • Renewal fails (card declined): invoice stays open, subscription → past_due, dunning schedule kicks in (smart retries + emails), entitlement grace period before suspension.
    • Annual plan refund on cancel: policy decision — usually no refund, access until period end. State the policy explicitly; don't silently assume.

    Likely follow-ups

    • "How do enterprise contracts differ?" Sales-negotiated price (custom prices row, not catalog), invoice with NET-30 terms instead of auto-charge, manual or ACH payment, possibly annual-up-front. Same invoice/ledger core, different collection path.
    • "How do you handle a price change for existing customers?" New price version; existing subs keep their old version until they're explicitly migrated. Never mutate a price in place.
    • "How do entitlements stay in sync?" Separate entitlements service/table derived from active subscription state; updated in the same txn as the seat change, or via an event the entitlements service consumes idempotently. (See the Entitlements problem below.)
    • "Scale 10×?" The bottleneck is the renewal burst at month boundaries, not steady QPS. Spread anniversary dates, shard the invoice cron by customer_id, make each invoice generation independent and idempotent so it can be retried/parallelized freely.

    ★★ Usage-Based Metering & Rating (the "design a metered billing pipeline" classic)

    Why likely: the canonical billing system-design problem, and increasingly relevant to Scribe as AI features (Optimize) push toward consumption pricing. Even if Scribe is seat-based today, "how would you add usage-based billing?" is a natural probe for a billing hire.

    Prompt: "Design a usage-based billing system. Customers emit usage events (e.g. AI guide generations, API calls). We meter usage, rate it against a price, and roll it into a monthly invoice. Handle high event volume, late/duplicate/out-of-order events, and retroactive corrections."

    Clarifying questions

    1. Event volume & latency? Real-time balance display, or is end-of-month accuracy enough? (Changes everything: real-time → stream + cache; batch → store-and-query.)
    2. Pricing model? Flat per-unit, tiered, volume, graduated? Do prices change retroactively?
    3. Accuracy bar? Must every event count exactly (it's money), or is sampling acceptable? (For billing: exact.)
    4. Do customers dispute usage? If yes, you need raw-event retention + replayability, not just aggregates.

    The 5-stage pipeline (name these)

      ingest ──▶ store (raw, immutable) ──▶ aggregate ──▶ rate (apply price) ──▶ invoice
    StageWhat happens
    IngestAccept events via API/SDK. Each carries a client-supplied idempotency_key / transaction_id. At-least-once; dedup makes it effectively exactly-once. Validate & enqueue fast; don't block the caller.
    StoreWrite raw events to an immutable, append-only store (columnar warehouse / event log). This is the source of truth. Never mutate.
    AggregateRoll events into billable quantities per (customer, metric, period).
    RateApply the price (tiers/volume) to aggregated quantity → money. Deterministic: same events + same price → same amount.
    InvoiceMaterialize rated amounts as invoice line items at period close.

    Common trap: "I'll pre-aggregate everything for performance." Sounds right, is usually wrong for billing — you lose clean backfills and retro-corrections. Lead with the correct framing; it's strong signal.

    Idempotent ingestion & late events

    • Dedup: unique constraint on (customer_id, idempotency_key). Retries/heartbeats use a deterministic key so duplicates collapse. Dedup window TTL must outlive the producer's max retry window.
    • Out-of-order: events carry an event_timestamp (when it happened) separate from ingest_timestamp (when we got it). Bill by event_timestamp; out-of-order is a non-issue if you store raw and query by event time.
    • Late events after invoice close: with query-time architecture, apply as a read-path overlay / true-up on the next invoice (a correction line item) rather than reopening a finalized invoice. Finalized invoices stay immutable.
    • Corrections / amendments: layer credits or adjustments on top; don't rewrite history.

    Failure modes

    • Duplicate event delivery: dedup key → counted once.
    • Event arrives 3 days late: stored by event_timestamp; included when that period is (re)queried; if period already invoiced → true-up next cycle.
    • Price changed retroactively: re-rate raw events against the corrected price; emit an adjustment. Possible only because raw events are retained.
    • Ingestion pipeline lag at month close: define a cutoff/grace window before finalizing; events after cutoff roll to next period. State the cutoff explicitly.

    ★★ Stripe Webhooks + Payment Reconciliation (your Zuora bug, generalized)

    Why this is YOUR problem: the Zendesk silent-200 duplicate-charge bug is exactly this surface — a third party returning success ambiguously, retries creating duplicates. You found and fixed it in production. Designing the system that makes it impossible is a story you can tell better than almost any candidate.

    Prompt: "We charge customers through Stripe and react to Stripe webhooks to update our billing state. Design this so our state is always correct despite duplicate, out-of-order, and lost webhooks."

    The webhook handling rules (do them IN THIS ORDER)

    1. Verify the HMAC signature on the raw request body. Any re-serialization of the body breaks the signature — verify before parsing.
    2. Dedup on the event id via a UNIQUE constraint in a processed_webhooks table. Stripe retries; the same event id can arrive many times.
    3. Enqueue + return 200 fast. Don't do heavy work in the handler — persist the event, ack, process async. Slow handlers cause Stripe to retry and pile up.
    4. Process async: REFETCH the resource from Stripe, don't trust the payload. Webhooks are unordered and can be stale; refetch the PaymentIntent and converge your state to it.

    The trap (and your real bug): treating the webhook (or a 200 from the PSP) as authoritative and writing state directly. A duplicate or out-of-order delivery then double-applies. The fix is the same shape as your Zendesk fix: idempotent processing keyed on a stable id, plus reconcile against the source of truth.

    "Webhooks are triggers, not truth"

    Stripe does not guarantee ordering or exactly-once delivery. So never build a state machine that assumes payment_intent.created arrives before .succeeded. Instead: a webhook is a nudge that says "something changed, go look." You refetch the canonical object and set your state from it. This makes ordering irrelevant and duplicates harmless.

    The reconciliation safety net (the part juniors skip)

    Webhooks get lost — endpoint down, deploy mid-delivery, Stripe gives up after retries. So you never rely on webhooks alone. A scheduled reconciliation job pulls recent PaymentIntents/charges from Stripe and compares them against your payment_attempts / invoices state, healing any drift (mark a stuck invoice paid, flag a mystery charge). Webhooks = low-latency happy path; reconciliation = guaranteed eventual correctness.

    The outbox tie-in (atomic state + side effect)

    When the webhook processor updates billing state AND needs to trigger something downstream (send a receipt, flip entitlement), it writes the state change + an outbox event in one DB transaction, never DB-then-publish. The classic dual-write trap: you mark the invoice paid, then crash before publishing "send receipt" — customer paid, no receipt. Outbox makes it atomic.

    Data model

    processed_webhooksstripe_event_id (UNIQUE), type, received_at, processed_at — the dedup table
    payment_attemptsid, invoice_id, stripe_payment_intent_id, status, amount_cents, idempotency_key (UNIQUE), created_at
    outbox_eventsid, topic, payload, published_at (NULL until published)

    Failure modes

    • Same webhook delivered 5×: UNIQUE on event id → processed once, the rest are no-ops.
    • succeeded arrives before created: we refetch the intent; current truth is "succeeded"; we converge. Order didn't matter.
    • Webhook never arrives: reconciliation job catches it within its cadence (e.g. hourly).
    • We charged but our "mark paid" write failed: webhook OR reconciliation refetches and converges. The charge (with idempotency key) is never repeated.
    • Stripe outage: charge worker retries with backoff; idempotency key ensures no double charge when it recovers.

    ★ Double-Entry Ledger (money-correctness core)

    Prompt: "Design the ledger that records all money movement in our billing system — charges, credits, refunds, proration adjustments — so balances are always correct and auditable."

    The core ideas (say all four)

    • Double-entry: every movement is two balanced postings — debit one account, credit another. The invariant debits = credits is enforced per transaction, so the books are always internally consistent.
    • Append-only / immutable: no UPDATE, no DELETE. A reversal is a new compensating entry. This is what makes it auditable and reconciliation tractable.
    • Balances are derived, not stored: balance = fold over the entry log (with periodic snapshot rows for performance). The log is the truth; the balance is a projection.
    • The entry id is the idempotency key: a retried "post this transfer" with the same id must not move money twice.

    Data model

    accountsid, owner (customer/revenue/cash/credits), currency, type
    ledger_transactionsid (idempotency key), description, created_at — groups the postings
    ledger_entriesid, transaction_id, account_id, direction (debit/credit), amount_cents, currency. SUM(debits)=SUM(credits) per transaction (enforced in app txn / constraint).
    balance_snapshotsaccount_id, as_of, balance_cents — periodic, to avoid folding the whole log

    Tradeoffs & senior signals

    • Multi-currency: never sum across currencies. An FX conversion is itself two postings through an FX/clearing account.
    • Strong consistency required: posting + balance check in one ACID transaction, single-shard. Never read a balance off a replica.
    • Build vs buy: Postgres with app-enforced invariants is the right start; mention purpose-built ledger DBs (TigerBeetle) as the scale evolution — "I'd reach for a dedicated ledger engine when posting throughput or correctness guarantees outgrow a single Postgres, not before."

    ★ Invoicing Service (lifecycle & immutability)

    Prompt: "Design the invoicing service — generate invoices from subscription/usage state, finalize them, collect payment, and handle corrections."

    Invoice lifecycle

    draft ──(finalize)──▶ open ──(payment succeeds)──▶ paid
  │                     │
  │ (editable here      ├──(issued in error)──▶ void
  │  only)              └──(owed but written off)──▶ uncollectible
    • Editable only in draft. Once finalized (open), amount/customer/line items lock — tax and legal need a stable record.
    • Corrections after finalize = credit notes, never edits. A credit note adjusts money owed without rewriting the original document.
    • void = invoice should never have been issued; uncollectible = legitimately owed but written off as bad debt. Different meanings — use precisely.
    • Idempotent generation: generating an invoice for (subscription, period) twice must produce one invoice — UNIQUE on (subscription_id, period_start).

    Senior verbalization: "Finalized invoices are immutable; every correction is a credit note. That keeps an auditable, tax-compliant trail and means I can always reconstruct exactly what a customer was told they owed and when."

    ★ Entitlements / Plan Service (access derived from billing state)

    Prompt: "How does the product know what a customer is allowed to do (seats, features, limits), and how does that stay in sync with billing?"

    • Separate entitlements from billing, but derive entitlements from subscription state. The product asks "can this user do X?" — that's a hot read path and must be fast (cache it), decoupled from invoice generation.
    • Source: active subscription + plan → entitlements (seat count, feature flags, usage limits). Recompute on subscription change; consume the change via an idempotent event or update in the same txn.
    • Optimistic provisioning: turn access on immediately on upgrade (before money settles); reconcile/revoke async if payment ultimately fails. Don't make users wait on a payment round-trip — "Make our users heroes."
    • Grace periods: on past_due, don't hard-cut access — grace window + dunning, then suspend. Product policy, encoded in the entitlements service.
    • Consistency: entitlements can be eventually consistent (a few seconds of lag on a feature flag is fine); the money behind it must be strongly consistent. Separate the two on purpose.

    ★ Webhook Delivery System — outbound (very likely general prompt)

    Prompt: "Design a system that reliably delivers webhooks from our platform to customer endpoints (e.g. Scribe notifying a customer's system when a guide is published, or a billing event fires)."

    Architecture

    The points that score

    • Queue-first: persist then enqueue — decouples production from delivery, absorbs spikes, survives a slow/down receiver.
    • At-least-once → receiver must be idempotent. Sign payloads with HMAC + a timestamp (anti-replay) and include a stable event id so customers can dedup.
    • Retries: exponential backoff + JITTER — jitter avoids a thundering herd hammering a recovering endpoint. ~3–7 attempts over hours; cap interval (6–12h), total window 1–3 days, then disable + alert.
    • DLQ + replay after the customer fixes their endpoint.
    • Per-endpoint isolation: one customer's dead endpoint must not back up everyone else's deliveries (per-destination queues / concurrency limits).
    • Status code nuance: retry 408 and 429 even though they're 4xx. Don't retry other 4xx (client config error).

    ★ Idempotency Layer (the pattern under everything)

    Prompt: "Design a generic idempotency layer for our write APIs so retries are safe."

    • Client sends an Idempotency-Key header (a UUID it generates per logical operation).
    • Server keeps (key, request_hash) → cached_response. Same key + same body → return cached response. Same key + different body → 422 (client reused a key = bug). New key → process fresh.
    • The critical part: record the idempotency result AND the business effect in the same DB transaction. If you do the work then separately record the key and crash between, a retry double-applies. Atomicity is the whole correctness argument.
    • Store: Postgres for money flows (durable, ~1ms — worth it). Redis for non-money (faster, acceptable to lose). TTL must outlive the client's max retry window (24h typical).
    • Side-effect idempotency: any external side effect (charge a card, send an email) needs its own idempotency token to the vendor — worker-level dedup alone doesn't cover a vendor call that fired before the crash.

    This is your home turf — tie it to the Zendesk bug: "The production bug I fixed was exactly an idempotency gap — a provider returned 200 ambiguously and its retry created a duplicate charge. Designing this layer is designing the thing that makes that class of bug impossible."

    1. Event log aggregator most-likely shape

    Grows live: v1 count actions per user → v2 skip malformed lines → v3 top-N users by total events. Narrate each step.

    Python 
    from collections import defaultdict, Counter

def count_actions(lines):
    """Each line: 'user,action'. Count actions
    per user; skip malformed lines."""
    counts = defaultdict(Counter)     # user -> {action: n}
    for line in lines:
        parts = line.strip().split(",")
        if len(parts) != 2:
            continue                  # malformed -> skip
        user, action = (p.strip() for p in parts)
        if not user or not action:
            continue
        counts[user][action] += 1
    return counts

def top_users(counts, n):             # top-N by total events
    totals = {u: sum(c.values()) for u, c in counts.items()}
    return sorted(totals, key=lambda u: (-totals[u], u))[:n]

    2. In-memory store with TTL class modeling

    v1 set/get/delete → v2 TTL with lazy expiry → v3 inject the clock so it's testable. That last move is your strongest seniority signal — say "I'll inject the clock so tests control time instead of sleeping."

    Python 
    import time

class KVStore:
    """In-memory store, optional TTL + lazy expiry.
    Clock injected for testability."""
    def __init__(self, clock=time.monotonic):
        self._clock = clock
        self._data = {}            # key -> (value, expires_at|None)

    def set(self, key, value, ttl=None):
        expires = self._clock() + ttl if ttl is not None else None
        self._data[key] = (value, expires)

    def get(self, key):
        item = self._data.get(key)
        if item is None:
            return None
        value, expires = item
        if expires is not None and self._clock() >= expires:
            del self._data[key]    # lazy expiry on read
            return None
        return value

    def delete(self, key):
        self._data.pop(key, None)

    The 4-step answer framework

    1. State current complexity precisely — time and space, separately. "Single pass, so O(n) time; space is O(k) for the distinct keys."
    2. Name the bottleneck — which line/structure dominates as data grows? Usually it's memory (holding everything) or a sort.
    3. Offer the next step up — pick from the toolbox below and say why it helps.
    4. Name the tradeoff — every optimization costs something (memory, accuracy, complexity, latency). Saying the cost out loud is the senior signal.

    Sample answer, all 4 steps in ~20 seconds (event-log problem): "Right now it's a single pass, so O(n) time where n is the number of events; space is O(k) for the k distinct users I track. At 1000× the bottleneck isn't CPU — it's memory, because the counts grow with distinct users. To scale I'd partition events by a hash of the user id, aggregate each shard independently, then merge — the MapReduce shape. The tradeoff is the added coordination complexity."

    Big-O crash course (Big-O = notation for how work grows as input grows; it drops constants and keeps the dominant term, so it describes the shape, not the exact runtime)

    Read O(f(n)) as: "for input of size n, roughly this many steps (time) or this much memory (space)." Two rules: drop constants (O(2n) is just O(n)) and keep only the dominant term (O(n + n²) is O(n²)).

    Time and space are separate budgets. A hashmap usually trades space for time: spend O(n) memory to turn an O(n²) nested-loop search into an O(n) one-pass scan. Naming both is what makes the answer sound senior.

    Stream / single passInput too big for memory? Process it item-by-item and keep only a running summary — never load the whole thing. Turns an O(n)-memory job into O(1) or O(k).
    Partition + parallelizeShard the data by a key (hash it into buckets), process each bucket independently / in parallel, then merge. This is the MapReduce shape and how you use many machines.

    Drill 1 — Event log aggregator

    • Current: count_actions is one pass → O(n) time; space is O(u·a) (users × distinct actions). top_users sorts m users → O(m log m).
    • "Make top-N faster": don't sort all m users — use a heap to pull the top N in O(m log n), a big win when n ≪ m (n much smaller than m).
    • "Handle 100× / doesn't fit in memory": the counting is already streaming — I never hold all lines, just the running totals. The memory bound is the cardinality (number of distinct users). If that explodes: partition by user-hash and aggregate shards in parallel (MapReduce), push it to a DB GROUP BY, or if approximate counts are OK, a count-min sketch.
    • Listen-for: it's already single-pass; memory (not time) is the real limit; "heap for top-N"; "partition to scale."

    Drill 2 — In-memory store with TTL

    • Current: get/set/delete are O(1) average (dict). But lazy expiry (only removing an expired key when someone reads it) means write-once-never-read keys linger in memory — a slow memory leak.
    • "Fix the memory leak": add active expiration — a background sweeper over a sample of keys, or a min-heap of (expires_at, key) so you can pop the soonest-to-expire cheaply. Tradeoff: the heap adds O(log n) per set and needs lazy deletion of stale heap entries.
    • "Handle 100× / multi-server": a single-process dict can't scale or survive a restart — this is exactly what Redis is (O(1) hash + TTL + active & lazy expiration + persistence). Shard by key-hash for horizontal scale.
    • "What about threads?": a plain dict isn't safe for compound ops under concurrency — add a lock, or per-shard locks to cut contention (a hot key can still bottleneck one shard).
    • Listen-for: O(1) ops; the lazy-expiry tradeoff and how to fix it; "this is Redis."

    Drill 3 — Idempotent dedup & money problems

    • Dedup: O(n) time, O(k) space for the seen set. At scale the set is unbounded → in production it's a UNIQUE DB constraint or a TTL cache; for massive streams, a Bloom filter (tradeoff: a false positive could drop a real event, so back it with a DB check — a Bloom filter never has false negatives, so "not present" is always trustworthy).
    • Proration / tiered pricing: already O(1) / O(tiers) — here the "optimization" conversation is about correctness at scale, not speed: integer-cents math, rounding once, and idempotency so a retry can't double-charge. Say that — it shows billing maturity.

    Drill 4 — Rate limiter (common practical-round shape)

    • Problem: allow at most limit requests per window seconds, per key. The sliding-window log below keeps timestamps and drops ones older than the window.
    • Current: allow is amortized O(1) (each timestamp is added once and removed once); space is O(requests in the window) per key.
    • "Memory at scale": storing every timestamp is wasteful. Switch to a token bucket (track just a token count + last-refill time → O(1) space per key) or a fixed-window counter. Tradeoff: token bucket allows short bursts; fixed windows can let 2× through at a boundary.
    • "Across many servers": per-process counters don't coordinate → move the counter into Redis (atomic increment + TTL), so all servers share one limit.
    • Listen-for: sliding window vs token bucket tradeoff; "distributed → Redis."
    Python 
    from collections import deque, defaultdict

class RateLimiter:
    """Allow `limit` requests per `window` seconds, per key."""
    def __init__(self, limit, window, clock):
        self.limit, self.window, self.clock = limit, window, clock
        self.hits = defaultdict(deque)   # key -> timestamps

    def allow(self, key):
        now = self.clock()
        q = self.hits[key]
        while q and q[0] <= now - self.window:   # drop old ones
            q.popleft()
        if len(q) < self.limit:
            q.append(now)
            return True
        return False
    Ruby

    Drill 5 — Data-transformation pipeline (parse → clean → aggregate → write)

    • Current: a naive version loads the whole input into a list, transforms, then writes → O(n) time and O(n) memory. Fine until the file is bigger than RAM.
    • "Handle a 50 GB file": make it streaming — read a line/record at a time with a generator, transform, write incrementally. Memory drops to O(1) (plus any running aggregate).
    • "Make it faster": the work per record is independent, so parallelize — chunk the input and map chunks across workers, then reduce. Watch for backpressure: if the writer is slower than the readers, bound the queue so memory doesn't blow up.
    • "It keeps failing halfway": make each record's write idempotent and checkpoint progress, so a retry resumes instead of duplicating.
    • Listen-for: "stream don't materialize," "parallel map + reduce," backpressure, idempotent retries.

    Drill 6 — Find duplicates / two-sum (the classic time-vs-space tradeoff)

    • The tradeoff to name out loud: the brute force checks every pair → O(n²) time, O(1) space. A hashmap remembers what you've seen → O(n) time, O(n) space. You spent memory to buy speed.
    • "What if you can't use extra memory?": sort first, then scan with two pointers → O(n log n) time, O(1) extra space. Slower than the hashmap but no memory — that's the dial.
    • Listen-for: you can state all three points on the time/space curve (n² / n+n / n log n) and pick based on the constraint.
    Python 
    def two_sum(nums, target):
    # hashmap: O(n) time, O(n) space, one pass
    seen = {}                          # value -> index
    for i, x in enumerate(nums):
        if target - x in seen:
            return (seen[target - x], i)
        seen[x] = i
    return None

    Drill 7 — Merge overlapping intervals (billing periods, proration windows)

    • Problem & complexity: merge overlapping ranges (e.g. consolidate overlapping subscription periods). Sort by start → O(n log n), then one sweep → O(n). Total O(n log n), dominated by the sort.
    • "Can you beat O(n log n)?": only if the input is already sorted (then it's O(n)), or starts come from a bounded integer range (counting/bucket sort → O(n)). Otherwise the sort is the floor.
    • Billing relevance: this is exactly how you detect overlapping charge windows or coalesce proration periods — worth naming the domain tie-in.
    • Listen-for: "sort then sweep," recognizing the sort dominates, and the already-sorted special case.

    Glossary of lingo & acronyms

    Every term used above, in one place. Drop these naturally and the optimization conversation sounds fluent.

    TermWhat it means
    Big-ONotation for how cost grows with input size n. Drops constants, keeps the dominant term. Describes the shape, not the exact time.
    AmortizedAverage cost per operation across many ops, even if one is occasionally expensive (e.g. array append = amortized O(1)).
    Time vs spaceTwo separate budgets: CPU steps vs memory used. Optimizations usually trade one for the other.
    CardinalityThe number of distinct values (e.g. distinct users). High cardinality = many unique keys = more memory.
    Throughput vs latencyThroughput = ops per second (volume). Latency = time for one op. Batching raises throughput but adds latency.
    p50 / p95 / p99Percentile latency. "p99 = 50ms" → 99% of requests finish within 50ms. Tail (p99) matters more than the average.
    MapReduceA pattern: split data into shards, process each in parallel (map), then combine results (reduce). The model behind partition + merge.
    Sharding / partitioningSplitting data across buckets or servers by a key, so each holds only a slice.
    Horizontal vs vertical scalingHorizontal = add more machines. Vertical = make one machine bigger. Horizontal scales further but adds coordination.
    IdempotencyDoing an operation twice has the same effect as doing it once. Essential for billing — a retry must not double-charge.
    TTLTime-To-Live: how long a stored/cached item stays valid before it expires.
    Lazy vs active expirationLazy = remove an expired item only when it's next touched. Active = a background job sweeps and removes expired items.
    LRU / LFUCache-eviction policies: when full, drop the Least Recently Used (or Least Frequently Used) item.
    Bloom filterCompact probabilistic set. Answers "definitely not present" or "probably present." Saves memory; can false-positive, never false-negative.
    HyperLogLog (HLL)Tiny structure that estimates how many distinct items you've seen (cardinality) using almost no memory. Approximate.
    Count-min sketchEstimates how often each item appears (frequencies) in fixed memory. Can overcount slightly, never undercount.
    Token bucketRate-limit method: a bucket refills tokens at a steady rate; each request spends one; empty = rejected. Allows short bursts.
    Sliding windowCounting/limiting over the most recent T seconds, continuously moving forward.
    BackpressureWhen a downstream stage is slower, it signals upstream to slow down so queues/memory don't blow up.
    Hot keyOne key taking a disproportionate share of traffic; can overload a single shard even when the rest are idle.
    RedisAn in-memory key-value store with O(1) ops, built-in TTL/expiration, and persistence. The production answer for "fast lookups + expiry."
    ACIDDatabase transaction guarantees: Atomic, Consistent, Isolated, Durable. Why a billing write either fully happens or not at all.
    Eventual consistencyReplicas may briefly disagree but converge over time — the tradeoff some distributed systems make for availability.

    • Tooling: Mixpanel (analytics), Cursor (AI coding).

    Stack implication for design rounds: meet them in the monolith on Postgres. Default to "I'd do this in Django/Postgres with transactions and constraints," and only reach for extra infra (Kafka, a separate service, a ledger DB) when you can name the boundary that earns it.

    Questions to Ask curiosity = signal; ask in every round

    For the hiring manager / billing engineers

    • "What does the billing team own end-to-end — subscriptions, invoicing, the ledger, entitlements, the Stripe integration, all of it?"
    • "What's the hardest correctness problem the billing team is wrestling with right now?"
    • "How much of subscription state lives in your DB vs Stripe? Are you source-of-truth or is Stripe driving?"
    • "What's the on-call experience like for billing? What kinds of incidents actually page you?"
    • "What's a billing project on the roadmap you're most excited about — usage-based pricing, enterprise invoicing, something else?"

    Billing / architecture deep cuts (shows depth)

    • "How do you handle idempotency and reconciliation with Stripe today — central middleware, per-flow, a reconciliation job?"
    • "Do you have a double-entry ledger, or is billing state derived from invoices + Stripe?"
    • "How do entitlements stay in sync with subscription changes — same transaction, events, polling?"
    • "With seats + enterprise contracts + per-org Optimize, how unified is the billing model under the hood vs special-cased per product?"
    • "As the monolith grows, is there appetite to extract billing into its own service, or is the boundary not worth it yet?"

    For the product partner

    • "What's the thing engineers do that makes your work easier — and the thing you wish more engineers understood about billing/monetization?"
    • "How early does engineering get pulled into pricing/packaging decisions?"
    • "What's the biggest monetization bet on the roadmap right now?"

    For the co-founder / VP

    • "Post Series C, how has the bar or the priorities shifted for engineering?"
    • "Where do you see Scribe in three years — and what part of the roadmap excites you most?"
    • "As you scale past 120 people toward doubling, what's the cultural thing you're most trying to protect?"
    • "What's a recent technical decision the team got wrong and changed? How did that play out?"

    The closing question (ask every round)

    ASK THIS EVERY ROUND

    "Based on what we discussed, do you have any hesitations about my fit for this role? I'd love a chance to address them before we wrap up."

    Signals confidence + self-awareness + coachability. Almost no one does it, and interviewers remember it.

    Questions NOT to ask

    • "What does Scribe do?" — Googleable; signals you didn't prep.
    • "How much vacation / is it remote?" — save for recruiter / offer stage.
    • "How long until I get promoted?" — reads transactional.
    • Anything yes/no — make it open-ended so you actually learn something.
